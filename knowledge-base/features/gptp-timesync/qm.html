gPTP Qualification Through a QM-Only Ethernet Switch

 
    Document ID: TSC-DD-003 | Revision: 1.0
    Date: 2026-07-14 | Classification: Internal Review  
 

1. Executive Summary

This document addresses the feasibility of qualifying gPTP (IEEE 802.1AS) time synchronization from a Grandmaster clock to an end station when the Ethernet switch in the path is classified as QM (Quality Management — non-safety) under ISO 26262. The core question: can a safety-qualified gPTP chain be established through a non-qualified, non-time-aware switch?

Conclusion: Yes, with constraints. The approach depends on the application's time accuracy requirements, the switch's traffic characteristics, and the safety integrity level (ASIL) required by the consuming function. With a single switch hop, the impact is typically manageable under light-to-moderate network load.

2. The Core Problem

2.1 gPTP and Time-Aware Bridges

gPTP (IEEE 802.1AS) relies on Time-Aware Bridges (TABs) — switches that implement IEEE 802.1AS — to maintain nanosecond-level accuracy. A TAB acts as either:

  • Transparent Clock (TC): measures residence time (switch delay) and corrects the Sync message in-place
  • Boundary Clock (BC): terminates the sync chain on one port and re-generates it on another

A non-802.1AS switch (QM, not time-aware) does not implement TC or BC functionality. It forwards frames with unknown, variable delay, which gPTP cannot compensate for:

  • Pdelay_Req / Pdelay_Resp messages are forwarded like any other multicast frame
  • Residence time through the switch is not measured or corrected
  • The end station sees an uncompensated delay with jitter

A QM switch means the component was not developed with any safety integrity measures. Using it in a safety-critical path requires a safety case that justifies the omission or demonstrates that the risk is acceptable.

4. Qualification Approaches

4.1 Approach 1: Classify gPTP as QM (Recommended)

If the application consuming the time does not require safety integrity, classify gPTP itself as QM. The QM switch in the path is then irrelevant — no safety chain is broken.

When to use: Time sync is used for diagnostics, logging, statistics, or non-safety functions.

Pros: Simplest path, no additional safety work required.

Cons: If the consumer later needs ASIL, this approach does not apply.

4.2 Approach 2: Bounded Jitter + Safety Assumption

If the consumer needs ASIL:

  1. Measure the actual switch jitter under worst-case load (burst traffic, full buffers, temperature extremes)
  2. Bound the worst-case jitter through network calculus or measurement-based analysis
  3. Determine if gPTP accuracy + jitter margin meets the consumer's time tolerance
  4. Document as a safety assumption: "Switch model X, under load condition Y, has max delay Z µs"

When to use: The consumer has moderate ASIL requirements and the accuracy budget can accommodate the jitter.

Pros: No hardware change needed.

Cons: Requires measurement effort, validation, and ongoing monitoring. Risk of unanticipated jitter under untested conditions.

4.3 Approach 3: Direct Connection — No Switch in Path

Eliminate the switch from the gPTP sync path. Connect the Grandmaster directly to the end station via a dedicated Ethernet link.

When to use: Tight accuracy requirements (e.g., sensor fusion requiring <1 µs), or when safety classification requires deterministic timing.

Pros: No jitter, full accuracy, no qualification dependency on switch.

Cons: Requires an additional Ethernet port and cabling. May not be feasible in space-constrained designs.

4.4 Approach 4: Boundary Clock Before QM Switch

Place an 802.1AS-capable Boundary Clock (safety-qualified) between the Grandmaster and the QM switch. The BC isolates the upstream safety chain from the downstream non-qualified segment.

When to use: The upstream Grandmaster must be ASIL-qualified, and the downstream switch remains QM.

Pros: Isolates the safety-qualified upstream segment. The QM switch carries regenerated sync (not the original Grandmaster chain).

Cons: Still has jitter through the QM switch. Adds cost and complexity of a BC device.

4.5 Approach 5: Software Compensation with Network Calculus

Use a software-based correction at the end station that models the known switch behavior.

  1. Characterize the switch's delay properties (minimum, maximum, distribution)
  2. Implement a delay model in the gPTP application
  3. Apply correction based on known traffic conditions
  4. Add a safety margin to the correction

When to use: The switch is the only non-qualified element and its behavior is well-characterized.

Pros: No hardware changes.

Cons: Software complexity, requires validation, risk of model inaccuracy.

5. Mitigation Strategies

5.1 Traffic Prioritization

  • Tag gPTP frames with VLAN PCP 7 (highest priority)
  • This reduces queuing jitter by giving PTP frames priority over other traffic
  • Effect: Jitter typically reduces from 50–200 µs to 1–10 µs on a capable switch

5.2 Sync Message Rate

5.3 Holdover with Graceful Degradation

  • Sync loss detected → within 125 ms
  • Holdover mode → up to 60 s (device-dependent)
  • Graceful degradation → escalate to FAULT
  • Safe state → application-defined fallback
